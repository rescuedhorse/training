Legal Framework & Protected Grounds

The laws that protect you

Provincial

Alberta Human Rights Act

Prohibits discrimination and harassment in employment, services, and tenancy. Administered by the Alberta Human Rights Commission (AHRC).

Provincial

OHS Act

Since 2018, requires employers to address workplace harassment and violence, and to maintain a written harassment prevention plan.

Federal

Canadian Human Rights Act

Applies to federally regulated workplaces (banks, airlines, telecoms, federal government). Administered by the Canadian Human Rights Commission.

Harassment & Discrimination Defined

Harassment

Harassment is a form of discrimination under the Alberta Human Rights Act. It occurs when unwelcome conduct related to a protected ground creates a hostile or offensive work environment. A single serious incident can constitute harassment — repeated conduct is not always required.

Examples of harassment

  • Racial slurs or jokes
  • Mocking someone's religion
  • Misgendering someone repeatedly after correction
  • Excluding someone due to a disability
  • Spreading rumours about sexual orientation

What harassment is NOT

  • Reasonable performance management
  • Legitimate criticism delivered respectfully
  • Disagreements unrelated to a protected ground
  • Ordinary workplace stress

Intent does not determine whether harassment occurred. If conduct is unwelcome and connected to a protected ground, it may be harassment even if the person meant no harm.

Discrimination

Discrimination is adverse treatment in employment because of a protected ground. It takes three forms:

TypeWhat it meansExample
DirectExplicit adverse treatmentRefusing to promote based on religion
IndirectNeutral policy with disproportionate impact“No headwear” rule affecting faith communities
SystemicEmbedded patterns disadvantaging a groupHiring criteria that screen out certain ethnicities

Discrimination in employment covers:

AreaExamples
HiringDiscriminatory interview questions; refusing to hire based on name or ethnicity
CompensationPaying less due to gender or disability
PromotionBypassing qualified candidates based on age or religion
TerminationFiring someone because of pregnancy, disability, or family status
Working conditionsAssigning inferior tasks based on protected grounds

Duty to accommodate

Employers must accommodate needs related to protected grounds (disability, religion, pregnancy, family status) up to the point of undue hardship. Employees must cooperate in finding workable solutions.

Sexual Harassment

Sexual harassment is gender-based harassment and discrimination, prohibited under the Alberta Human Rights Act and the OHS Act. It can occur between any genders, in any direction — peer-to-peer, manager-to-employee, or employee-to-manager.

Quid pro quo

“This for that”

A person in authority makes employment decisions (hiring, scheduling, promotion) conditional on sexual favours.

Hostile environment

Persistent unwelcome conduct

Conduct severe or pervasive enough to create an intimidating, hostile, or abusive work environment.

Examples include

  • Unwanted touching or physical contact
  • Sexual comments, jokes, or innuendo
  • Displaying or sending sexual images or messages
  • Repeated unwanted romantic overtures after a clear refusal

Victims are never responsible for harassment directed at them. Retaliation against someone who reports sexual harassment is itself a human rights violation. A victim's silence or discomfort — without a verbal objection — still indicates the conduct is unwelcome.

A harassment-free workplace is a legal right. Victims are never responsible for harassment directed at them. Retaliation against someone who reports sexual harassment is itself a violation of human rights law.

Scenario

A supervisor tells a team member they will receive a shift preference “if they're friendly enough.” The employee feels uncomfortable but says nothing and avoids the supervisor. This is quid pro quo sexual harassment — the employee's discomfort, even without a verbal objection, indicates the conduct is unwelcome.

SECTION 4 OF 10

Rights & Responsibilities

All employees have the right to:

  • Work free from harassment and discrimination
  • File a complaint without fear of retaliation
  • Request accommodation for protected grounds
  • Know your employer's harassment prevention policy
  • Participate in investigations as a witness

All employees are responsible to:

  • Treat colleagues with dignity and respect
  • Report harassment or discrimination they experience or witness
  • Cooperate with workplace investigations
  • Participate in accommodation processes
  • Complete required training

Employer and supervisor obligations

Employers must maintain a harassment-free workplace and — under the OHS Act for organizations with 20+ employees — maintain a written harassment prevention plan. Supervisors must model respectful conduct, address concerns brought to them, avoid retaliating against those who report, and promptly escalate complaints. Employers can be vicariously liable for harassment by supervisors or staff if they knew or ought to have known and failed to act.

Bystander Responsibility

If you witness harassment or discrimination, you have both a moral and a legal obligation to respond. Remaining silent or passive when you observe misconduct can enable it to continue.

The bystander's role

Alberta's OHS Act recognizes that workers have a responsibility to report unsafe conditions — including harassment — that they observe. You do not have to wait for the victim to act. Reporting what you have witnessed, even confidentially, is both permitted and encouraged.

Direct

Speak up in the moment

If safe to do so, name what you are seeing: “That comment was inappropriate and I think you should apologize.” Even a brief, calm statement can halt the behaviour.

Delegate

Involve someone else

Alert a supervisor, HR, or another trusted person. You do not need to handle it alone.

Delay

Check in afterwards

If you cannot act in the moment, check in with the person who was targeted. Ask if they are okay and whether they would like support in reporting.

Document

Record what happened

Write down the date, time, location, what was said or done, and who was present. This can be critical evidence in an investigation.

Retaliation against someone for reporting harassment — or for being a witness — is illegal under the Alberta Human Rights Act. You are protected for speaking up.

Scenario

You overhear a colleague making repeated mocking comments about a coworker's accent during a team lunch. The targeted colleague looks uncomfortable but says nothing. What should you do? Document what you observed, check in with your colleague afterwards, and consider reporting to management even if the colleague does not.

Reporting

If you experience, witness, or have knowledge of a workplace incident, you are encouraged to take the following steps.

Step 1

Address the conduct directly (if comfortable)

If you feel safe and comfortable doing so, make it known to the person that their conduct is unwelcome and ask them to stop. You are never required to confront someone directly, and you should only do this if you feel it is safe.

Step 2

Report internally

Report any incidents to your supervisor or another member of the management team. Incidents should be directed to:

  • Department Head
  • Producer
  • Donna Serafinus at Seven24 Films — donna@seven24films.com
  • Confidential reporting: heartlandconfidential@gmail.com
Step 3 — Last resort

External complaint (only after exhausting internal avenues)

If you have reported the matter internally, notified the company that it remains unresolved, and have not received a response, you may as a last resort file a complaint with an external body:

  • Alberta Human Rights Commission (AHRC) — within one year of the last incident — 1-800-232-7215 | ahrc.ca
  • Alberta Occupational Health & Safety (OHS) — if the employer has failed to meet its OHS obligations
  • Canadian Human Rights Commission — for federally regulated employees, within one year of the last incident

No retaliation: Reporting an incident or acting as a witness is fully protected. Any retaliation — demotion, termination, or ostracism — is itself a human rights violation.

SECTION 7 OF 10

What Happens After a Complaint

Understanding the process can reduce anxiety about coming forward. Here is what to expect after an incident is reported internally.

Internal process

Workplace investigation

The employer assigns an investigator (internal or external). Both parties are interviewed and can provide evidence, and witnesses, if applicable. The investigator produces findings and recommendations.

Possible remedies

TypeExamples
Non-monetaryApology; policy changes; training requirements; reinstatement
OrganizationalMandatory anti-harassment training; compliance monitoring

Consequences for individuals who harass

Depending on severity: verbal or written warning, mandatory training, demotion, transfer, suspension, or termination. In extreme cases involving assault or threats, criminal charges may also be pursued independently of the workplace process.

Complaints made in good faith that are not substantiated are not considered false complaints — the outcome of an investigation does not determine whether the person acted in good faith. False or malicious complaints are, however, themselves a policy violation.

Anti-Racism in the Workplace

Anti-racism goes beyond simply not being racist. It is an active, ongoing commitment to identifying, challenging, and changing the values, structures, and behaviours that perpetuate systemic racism — in our workplaces and in ourselves.

What racism looks like at work

  • Racial slurs, jokes, or stereotyping
  • Exclusion or isolation based on race or colour
  • Assumptions about ability or competence tied to ethnicity
  • Dismissing someone's experience of racism
  • Policies or practices that disadvantage racialized groups

What anti-racism looks like

  • Speaking up when you witness racist conduct
  • Listening to and believing the experiences of racialized colleagues
  • Examining your own assumptions and biases
  • Advocating for equitable practices in hiring, promotion, and recognition
  • Continuing to learn — anti-racism is a practice, not a destination

The difference between “not racist” and “anti-racist”: Being “not racist” is passive — it means avoiding racist behaviour yourself. Being anti-racist is active — it means taking deliberate steps to challenge racism when you encounter it, whether in individual conduct, team dynamics, or organizational practices.

Understanding unconscious bias

Unconscious bias refers to attitudes or stereotypes that affect our decisions and actions without our awareness. Everyone carries biases shaped by culture, media, and experience. In the workplace, unconscious bias can affect who gets hired, praised, mentored, or promoted.

Affinity bias

Favouring people who are similar to us in background, appearance, or experience.

Attribution bias

Crediting successes and failures differently depending on a person's race or background.

Microaggressions

Brief, everyday exchanges that send denigrating messages to members of marginalized groups — often unintentional but genuinely harmful.

Microaggressions are not “minor”: Comments like “You speak such good English” or “Where are you really from?” may seem harmless to the person saying them, but they communicate that someone is perceived as foreign or “other.” Cumulative exposure to microaggressions causes real psychological harm and contributes to a hostile work environment.

Our commitment as a workplace

We are committed to building a workplace where all people — regardless of race, colour, ancestry, or place of origin — are treated with dignity, have equitable access to opportunities, and feel genuinely included. This commitment belongs to every person on our team, not only to those who have experienced racism.

What you can do

  • Educate yourself — seek out resources on anti-racism; do not rely solely on racialized colleagues to educate you
  • Amplify marginalized voices — ensure the contributions and ideas of racialized colleagues are heard and credited
  • Interrupt bias — when you notice biased language, assumptions, or decisions, say something respectfully
  • Support those who report — if a colleague reports a racist incident, believe them and offer support
  • Reflect regularly — consider how your own decisions at work may be influenced by bias
Scenario

During a meeting, a colleague presents an idea that is met with silence. Ten minutes later, another colleague — who is white — says essentially the same thing and receives enthusiastic agreement. The first colleague, who is a person of colour, notices this pattern regularly. This is a form of racial bias. An anti-racist response is to name what happened: “I want to go back — [name] suggested that idea earlier and I think it deserves a response.”
